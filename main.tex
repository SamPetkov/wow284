\begin{abstract}
WOW-284 asserts that the minimum dual degree of every connected graph of
order at least three and girth at least five is at most the negative of its
least distance eigenvalue.  We record a counterexample on 50 vertices.  The
graph is given by an explicit coordinate construction over \(\mathbb F_5\).
It is 7-regular, has girth five, and has distance spectrum
\[
  \{91^{(1)},1^{(21)},(-4)^{(28)}\}.
\]
Consequently its minimum dual degree is \(7\), whereas the negative of its
least distance eigenvalue is \(4\).  The strict gap is therefore \(3\).  The
graph is the Hoffman--Singleton graph, but no classification or named-graph
property enters the proof.  A coordinate common-neighbor certificate yields
connectedness and girth, and a matrix calculation gives the full spectrum.
Exact code constructs the distance matrix directly by breadth-first search,
without using the analytic distance identity, and checks its characteristic
polynomial over the integers.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Introduction}

Let \(G\) be a finite simple connected graph on \(n\ge2\) vertices.  Its
distance matrix is \(D(G)=(d_G(u,v))_{u,v\in V(G)}\), where \(d_G\) is graph
distance.  This matrix is real symmetric; order its real eigenvalues as
\[
  \partial_1(G)\ge \partial_2(G)\ge\cdots\ge\partial_n(G).
\]
For a vertex \(v\), its \emph{dual degree} is the average degree of its
neighbors,
\[
  d^*(v)=\frac{1}{d(v)}\sum_{u\in N(v)}d(u),
  \qquad
  \delta^*(G)=\min_{v\in V(G)}d^*(v).
\]
Write \(g(G)\) for the shortest-cycle length, with \(g(G)=\infty\) for an
acyclic graph; spectral superscripts indicate algebraic multiplicity.

Aouchiche and Hansen record the following Graffiti conjecture in their survey
of distance spectra \cite[Conjecture~7.16, p.~370]{AouchicheHansen2014}; they
attribute it to Fajtlowicz's 1998 \emph{Written on the Wall} report
\cite{Fajtlowicz1998}.  For related historical context, see DeLaVi\~na's
separate \emph{Written on the Wall II} catalogue \cite{DeLaVinaWOWII}.

\begin{conjecture}[WOW-284]\label{conj:wow284}
If \(G\) is connected, has \(n\ge3\) vertices, and has girth
\(g(G)\ge5\), then
\[
  \delta^*(G)\le -\partial_n(G).
\]
\end{conjecture}

In that 2014 survey, the authors reported that the conjecture remained open
and noted equality for the Petersen graph
\cite[Conjecture~7.16, p.~370]{AouchicheHansen2014}.  The degree-7 Moore graph
constructed by Hoffman and Singleton \cite{HoffmanSingleton1960} gives a
strict violation.

There is an important prior spectral calculation.  Howlader and Panigrahi
explicitly give the distance spectrum
\(\{91,(-4)^{(28)},1^{(21)}\}\) of the Hoffman--Singleton graph
\cite[Theorem~2.5(1b)]{HowladerPanigrahi2022}.  Combining that calculation
with 7-regularity already disproves Conjecture~\ref{conj:wow284}.  The present
note records this connection and supplies a self-contained coordinate
certificate, so neither the graph identification nor the earlier spectral
formula is needed as a premise.  No claim is made that the spectrum is new,
that this observation has priority over every unpublished observation, or
that 50 is the smallest possible order.

\begin{theoremA}
There is a simple connected graph \(G\) on 50 vertices such that
\[
  g(G)=5,\qquad \delta^*(G)=7,\qquad \partial_{50}(G)=-4.
\]
In particular, \(G\) violates WOW-284 by the strict gap
\(\delta^*(G)+\partial_{50}(G)=3\).
\end{theoremA}

\section{Coordinate construction}

All subscripts in this section lie in the field
\(\F=\mathbb Z/5\mathbb Z\).  Introduce vertices
\[
 V(G)=\{P_{i,j}:i,j\in\F\}\mathbin{\dot\cup}
      \{Q_{k,\ell}:k,\ell\in\F\}.
\]
Thus \(|V(G)|=50\).  The edges are the following unordered pairs:
\begin{align*}
 E_P&=\bigl\{\{P_{i,j},P_{i,j+1}\}:i,j\in\F\bigr\},\\
 E_Q&=\bigl\{\{Q_{k,\ell},Q_{k,\ell+2}\}:k,\ell\in\F\bigr\},\\
 E_X&=\bigl\{\{P_{i,j},Q_{k,ik+j}\}:i,j,k\in\F\bigr\}.
\end{align*}
Set \(E(G)=E_P\cup E_Q\cup E_X\).  For a fixed numerical labeling, with
representatives in \(\{0,1,2,3,4\}\), use
\[
 P_{i,j}\longleftrightarrow 5i+j,
 \qquad
 Q_{k,\ell}\longleftrightarrow 25+5k+\ell.
\]

After reindexing the \(Q\)-layers by \(k\mapsto-k\), these are exactly
Hafner's affine-coordinate form of Robertson's pentagon-and-pentagram
construction \cite[Section~2 and Theorem~2.1]{Hafner2003}.  Thus \(G\) is the
Hoffman--Singleton graph; all properties needed here are proved directly.

The complete neighborhood formulas are
\begin{align}
 N(P_{i,j})
 &=\{P_{i,j-1},P_{i,j+1}\}
   \cup\{Q_{k,ik+j}:k\in\F\},\label{eq:p-neighborhood}\\
 N(Q_{k,\ell})
 &=\{Q_{k,\ell-2},Q_{k,\ell+2}\}
   \cup\{P_{i,\ell-ik}:i\in\F\}.
   \label{eq:q-neighborhood}
\end{align}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:simple-regular}
The construction defines a simple 7-regular graph with 175 edges.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Every listed edge has distinct endpoints.  Within each of the five fixed
\(P\)-layers, addition by \(1\) gives a 5-cycle; within each fixed
\(Q\)-layer, addition by \(2\) gives a 5-cycle.  The cross edges have one
endpoint of each type.  The set notation removes any possibility of parallel
edges.

Equations~\eqref{eq:p-neighborhood}--\eqref{eq:q-neighborhood} list seven
distinct neighbors at every vertex.  There are 25 edges of type \(E_P\), 25
of type \(E_Q\), and 125 cross edges, hence 175 in total.  Equivalently, the
handshake identity gives \(50\cdot7/2=175\).
\end{proof}

\section{The common-neighbor certificate}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:common-neighbors}
For any two distinct vertices \(x,y\),
\[
 |N(x)\cap N(y)|=
 \begin{cases}
 0,&x\sim y,\\
 1,&x\not\sim y.
 \end{cases}
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
First consider two \(P\)-vertices \(P_{i,j}\) and \(P_{i',j'}\).  If
\(i=i'\), their layer is a 5-cycle.  Adjacent pairs have no common
\(P\)-neighbor, while nonadjacent pairs have exactly one.  They cannot have a
common \(Q\)-neighbor: for fixed \(i\) and \(k\), the equation
\(Q_{k,\ell}\sim P_{i,j}\) determines \(j=\ell-ik\) uniquely.  If
\(i\ne i'\), there is no common \(P\)-neighbor, and a common \(Q\)-neighbor
must satisfy
\[
 ik+j=i'k+j'.
\]
This has the unique solution
\[
 k=\frac{j'-j}{i-i'},\qquad \ell=ik+j,
\]
because \(\F\) is a field.

The argument for two \(Q\)-vertices is dual.  If their first coordinates
agree, they lie in the 5-cycle generated by steps \(\pm2\).  Adjacent pairs
have no common \(Q\)-neighbor and nonadjacent pairs have exactly one; no
common \(P\)-neighbor is possible.  If \(k\ne k'\), a common
\(P_{i,j}\) is determined uniquely by
\[
 \ell=ik+j,\qquad \ell'=ik'+j,
\]
namely
\[
 i=\frac{\ell-\ell'}{k-k'},\qquad j=\ell-ik.
\]

It remains to compare \(P_{i,j}\) with \(Q_{k,\ell}\).  Put
\[
 r=\ell-(ik+j)\in\F.
\]
The pair is adjacent precisely when \(r=0\).  A common \(P\)-vertex must be
\(P_{i,j+s}\) with \(s\in\{\pm1\}\), and it is adjacent to
\(Q_{k,\ell}\) precisely when \(r=s\).  Hence there is exactly one common
\(P\)-neighbor if \(r\in\{\pm1\}\), and none otherwise.  A common
\(Q\)-vertex must be \(Q_{k,\ell+t}\) with \(t\in\{\pm2\}\), and it is
adjacent to \(P_{i,j}\) precisely when \(t=-r\).  Hence there is exactly one
common \(Q\)-neighbor if \(r\in\{\pm2\}\), and none otherwise.  Finally,
\[
 \F=\{0\}\mathbin{\dot\cup}\{\pm1\}
          \mathbin{\dot\cup}\{\pm2\},
\]
which proves every cross case and completes the certificate.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:geometry}
The graph is connected, has diameter two, and has girth five.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Every pair of distinct vertices is adjacent or has a common neighbor, so the
graph is connected and has diameter at most two.  It is not complete, since
it is 7-regular on 50 vertices, so its diameter is exactly two.

A triangle would give an adjacent pair a common neighbor.  In a 4-cycle, two
opposite vertices would have the other two cycle vertices as distinct common
neighbors.  Both possibilities contradict
Proposition~\ref{prop:common-neighbors}.  Thus \(g(G)\ge5\).  On the other
hand, for every fixed \(i\),
\[
 P_{i,0}P_{i,1}P_{i,2}P_{i,3}P_{i,4}P_{i,0}
\]
is a 5-cycle, so \(g(G)=5\).
\end{proof}

\section{Exact spectral calculation}

Let \(A\) be the adjacency matrix, let \(I\) be the \(50\times50\) identity
matrix, and let \(J\) be the all-ones matrix.  Proposition
\ref{prop:common-neighbors} gives, entry by entry,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:a-square}
 A^2=6I-A+J.
\end{equation}
Indeed, the diagonal entries on both sides are 7, the entries indexed by
edges are 0, and all other off-diagonal entries are 1.

Since the graph is 7-regular, \(A\one=7\one\).  This eigenvalue is simple:
if \(Ax=7x\), then
\[
  0=x^{\mathsf T}(7I-A)x
   =\sum_{\{u,v\}\in E(G)}(x_u-x_v)^2,
\]
so connectedness forces \(x\) to be constant.  Since \(A\) is real symmetric,
\(\one^\perp\) is \(A\)-invariant and has an orthonormal eigenbasis.  On this
subspace, equation~\eqref{eq:a-square} becomes
\[
 A^2+A-6I=0.
\]
Thus every other adjacency eigenvalue is \(2\) or \(-3\).  If their
multiplicities are \(m_2\) and \(m_{-3}\), then
\[
 m_2+m_{-3}=49,
 \qquad
 7+2m_2-3m_{-3}=\tr A=0,
\]
where \(\tr A=0\) because the graph is loopless.
Consequently
\begin{equation}\label{eq:adjacency-spectrum}
 \Spec(A)=\{7^{(1)},2^{(28)},(-3)^{(21)}\}.
\end{equation}

By Corollary~\ref{cor:geometry}, two distinct vertices are at distance one on
edges and distance two on nonedges.  Therefore
\begin{equation}\label{eq:distance-matrix}
 D=A+2(J-I-A)=2J-2I-A.
\end{equation}
On \(\one\), the distance matrix has eigenvalue
\[
 2\cdot50-2-7=91.
\]
If \(x\in\one^\perp\) and \(Ax=\theta x\), then
\[
 Dx=(-2I-A)x=(-2-\theta)x.
\]
Using \eqref{eq:adjacency-spectrum}, we obtain
\begin{equation}\label{eq:distance-spectrum}
 \Spec(D)=\{91^{(1)},1^{(21)},(-4)^{(28)}\},
\end{equation}
or equivalently
\[
 \det(xI-D)=(x-91)(x-1)^{21}(x+4)^{28}.
\]

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem A]
Corollary~\ref{cor:geometry} gives connectedness and \(g(G)=5\).  By
Lemma~\ref{lem:simple-regular}, every vertex and every one of its neighbors
has degree 7, so
\[
 d^*(v)=\frac{1}{7}\sum_{u\in N(v)}7=7
\]
for every \(v\), and hence \(\delta^*(G)=7\).  Equation
\eqref{eq:distance-spectrum} gives \(\partial_{50}(G)=-4\).  Thus
\[
 \delta^*(G)=7>4=-\partial_{50}(G),
 \qquad
 \delta^*(G)+\partial_{50}(G)=3>0.
\]
This is a strict counterexample to WOW-284.
\end{proof}

Equivalently, \(D+\delta^*(G)I=D+7I\) has eigenvalues
\(98\), \(8\) with multiplicity 21, and \(3\) with multiplicity 28.  Thus
it is positive definite, which is the matrix form of the strict inequality
\(\delta^*(G)>-\partial_{50}(G)\).

\section{Computational certificate}

The proof above is entirely analytic.  The accompanying Python code supplies
a separate exact check from the coordinate rules.  It performs the following
steps:
\begin{enumerate}
\item constructs the 175 unordered edges and checks all 50 degrees;
\item checks the common-neighbor count for every unordered vertex pair;
\item constructs all 2,500 distance entries by integer breadth-first search;
\item computes the distance characteristic polynomial symbolically; and
\item computes an exact rational \(LDL^{\mathsf T}\) decomposition of
      \(D+7I\) and checks that all 50 pivots are positive.
\end{enumerate}
The symbolic steps use SymPy \cite{MeurerEtAl2017}.  The expected output is
\[
  (x-91)(x-1)^{21}(x+4)^{28}
\]
together with 50 positive rational pivots.  Because the distance matrix is
built by BFS, this check does not use equation~\eqref{eq:distance-matrix} or
the spectral derivation above.  Numerical agreement is not treated as a
proof; all calculations in the certificate are exact.
