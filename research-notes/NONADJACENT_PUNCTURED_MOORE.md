# Distance spectrum after deleting two nonadjacent Moore vertices

**Status:** proved for a hypothetical or realizable degree-`k` Moore graph with
`k\ge5`. The quotient algebra and the `k=7` Hoffman--Singleton specialization
are checked by `scripts/verify_nonadjacent_punctured_moore.py`. Literature
priority is not asserted.

## Theorem

Let `M` be a degree-`k` Moore graph of diameter two, where `k\ge5`. Let
`u,v` be nonadjacent vertices and put

\[
 H=M-\{u,v\}.
\]

Set

\[
 \Delta=\sqrt{4k-3},
\]

and define

\[
\begin{aligned}
 R_k(x)={}&x^4+(10-2k^2)x^3
 +(2k^3-17k^2-2k+36)x^2\\
 &+(12k^3-49k^2-4k+53)x\\
 &-2k^4+17k^3-38k^2+5k+20.
\end{aligned}
\]

Further put

\[
 M_-=
 \frac{k(k-2)+(k^2-4k+2)\Delta}{2\Delta},
\]

\[
 M_+=
 \frac{-k(k-2)+(k^2-4k+2)\Delta}{2\Delta}.
\]

Then the distance characteristic polynomial of `H` is

\[
\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
 \chi_{D(H)}(x)={}&(x-k+3)R_k(x)\\
 &\cdot(x^2+4x-k+3)^{k-2}\\
 &\cdot(x^2+4x-k+5)^{k-2}\\
 &\cdot\left(x+\frac{\Delta+3}{2}\right)^{M_-}\\
 &\cdot\left(x-\frac{\Delta-3}{2}\right)^{M_+}.
\end{aligned}
}
\]

The minimum dual degree is

\[
 \boxed{\delta^*(H)=k-\frac2k.}
\]

The full factorization does not in general identify the least quotient root by
a simple radical formula. Independently, the deletion-stability theorem proves
that `H` is a strict WOW-284 counterexample for every integer `k\ge6` for
which the Moore graph exists.

## 1. The five-cell geometry

The vertices `u,v` have a unique common neighbor `w`. Define

\[
 A=N(u)\setminus\{w\},
 \qquad
 B=N(v)\setminus\{w\},
\]

\[
 C=N(w)\setminus\{u,v\},
\]

and let `Z` contain the remaining vertices. Their sizes are

\[
 1,\quad k-1,\quad k-1,\quad k-2,
 \quad (k-1)(k-2).
\]

The Moore common-neighbor rule gives the following adjacency counts.

- `w` is adjacent to all vertices of `C` and to no vertex of `A\cup B\cup Z`.
- The edges between `A` and `B` form a perfect matching.
- Every vertex of `A` and of `B` has `k-2` neighbors in `Z`.
- Every vertex of `C` has `k-1` neighbors in `Z`.
- Every vertex of `Z` has one neighbor in each of `A,B,C` and `k-3`
  neighbors in `Z`.

Thus the partition

\[
 \{w\}\sqcup A\sqcup B\sqcup C\sqcup Z
\]

is equitable for the adjacency matrix.

Every surviving pair has its original distance except pairs contained in
`N(u)` or in `N(v)`. Those pairs had the deleted vertex as their unique common
neighbor and now have distance three. Hence

\[
 D(H)=2(J-I)-A(H)+F,
\]

where `F` is the adjacency matrix of two copies of `K_k` sharing the vertex
`w`, on vertex sets `\{w\}\cup A` and `\{w\}\cup B`.

## 2. The constant-on-cell quotient

The row-sum quotient of `D(H)` for the five cells is

\[
 Q=
\begin{pmatrix}
0&3k-3&3k-3&k-2&2(k-1)(k-2)\\
3&3k-6&2k-3&2k-4&2k^2-7k+6\\
3&2k-3&3k-6&2k-4&2k^2-7k+6\\
1&2k-2&2k-2&2k-6&2k^2-7k+5\\
2&2k-3&2k-3&2k-5&2k^2-7k+5
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

The vector that is `1` on `A`, `-1` on `B`, and zero elsewhere has quotient
eigenvalue `k-3`. Direct determinant expansion gives

\[
 \det(xI-Q)=(x-k+3)R_k(x).
\]

These account for five distance eigenvalues with multiplicity.

## 3. Incidence identities

Identify `A` and `B` through their perfect matching. Let `R_A,R_B,R_C` be
the incidence matrices from `A,B,C` to `Z`, and let `T` be the adjacency
matrix induced on `Z`. The Moore identity

\[
 A(M)^2=(k-1)I-A(M)+J
\]

gives

\[
 R_AR_A^{\mathsf T}=R_BR_B^{\mathsf T}=(k-2)I,
\]

\[
 R_CR_C^{\mathsf T}=(k-1)I,
\]

\[
 R_AR_B^{\mathsf T}=J-I,
 \qquad
 R_AR_C^{\mathsf T}=R_BR_C^{\mathsf T}=J,
\]

and

\[
 R_AT+R_B=J-R_A,
\]

\[
 R_BT+R_A=J-R_B,
\]

\[
 R_CT=J-R_C.
\]

The bottom-right block also gives

\[
 R_A^{\mathsf T}R_A+R_B^{\mathsf T}R_B
 +R_C^{\mathsf T}R_C+T^2
 =(k-1)I-T+J.
\]

## 4. The two matched-neighborhood modules

Let

\[
 U=\{x\in\mathbb R^{A}:x\perp\mathbf1\},
 \qquad \dim U=k-2.
\]

For `x\in U`, put

\[
 p=R_A^{\mathsf T}x,
 \qquad
 q=R_B^{\mathsf T}x.
\]

The vectors `p+q` and `p-q` are nonzero for `k\ge5`, and

\[
 T(p+q)=-2(p+q),
 \qquad
 T(p-q)=0.
\]

On the symmetric module generated by

\[
 (0,x,x,0,0),
 \qquad
 (0,0,0,0,p+q),
\]

the distance matrix is represented by

\[
 \begin{pmatrix}
 -4&-(k-3)\\
 -1&0
 \end{pmatrix}.
\]

Its characteristic polynomial is

\[
 x^2+4x-k+3.
\]

On the antisymmetric module generated by

\[
 (0,x,-x,0,0),
 \qquad
 (0,0,0,0,p-q),
\]

the representing matrix is

\[
 \begin{pmatrix}
 -2&-(k-1)\\
 -1&-2
 \end{pmatrix},
\]

with characteristic polynomial

\[
 x^2+4x-k+5.
\]

Each quadratic factor therefore occurs with multiplicity `k-2`.

## 5. The common-neighbor module

For

\[
 y\in\mathbb R^C,
 \qquad y\perp\mathbf1,
\]

put `r=R_C^{\mathsf T}y`. The corresponding two-dimensional module carries

\[
 \begin{pmatrix}
 -2&-(k-1)\\
 -1&-1
 \end{pmatrix}.
\]

Its characteristic polynomial is

\[
 x^2+3x+3-k
 =\left(x+\frac{\Delta+3}{2}\right)
  \left(x-\frac{\Delta-3}{2}\right).
\]

Each of these two eigenvalues occurs `k-3` times on this module.

## 6. The residual `Z`-kernel

Let

\[
 K=\ker R_A\cap\ker R_B\cap\ker R_C
 \subseteq\mathbb R^Z.
\]

The constant vector, the symmetric and antisymmetric `A/B` images, and the
`C` image account for `3k-6` dimensions inside `\mathbb R^Z`. Hence

\[
 \dim K=(k-1)(k-2)-(3k-6)=(k-2)(k-4).
\]

On `K`, the bottom-right block identity reduces to

\[
 T^2+T-(k-1)I=0.
\]

Thus the two possible `T`-eigenvalues are

\[
 \frac{-1+\Delta}{2},
 \qquad
 \frac{-1-\Delta}{2}.
\]

The trace of `T` on `K` is `2(k-2)`. Solving the dimension and trace
equations gives multiplicities

\[
 \frac{(k-2)(k+(k-4)\Delta)}{2\Delta}
\]

and

\[
 \frac{(k-2)((k-4)\Delta-k)}{2\Delta}.
\]

Since `D=-2I-T` on `K`, these give distance eigenvalues

\[
 -\frac{\Delta+3}{2},
 \qquad
 \frac{\Delta-3}{2}.
\]

Adding the `k-3` copies from the common-neighbor module yields `M_-` and
`M_+` in the theorem.

All subspaces together have dimension

\[
 5+4(k-2)+M_-+M_+=k^2-1.
\]

This proves the complete factorization.

## 7. Minimum dual degree

The common neighbor `w` has degree `k-2`, the vertices in `A\cup B` have
degree `k-1`, and all other vertices have degree `k`.

- Every neighbor of `w` has degree `k`, so `d^*(w)=k`.
- A vertex in `A\cup B` has one neighbor of degree `k-1` and `k-2`
  neighbors of degree `k`, so its dual degree is

  \[
  k-\frac1{k-1}.
  \]

- A vertex in `C` or `Z` has neighbor-degree sum `k^2-2`, so its dual degree
  is

  \[
  k-\frac2k.
  \]

For `k>2`, the last value is the minimum.

## 8. Hoffman--Singleton specialization

For `k=7`, `\Delta=5`, and the factorization becomes

\[
\begin{aligned}
 \chi_{D(H)}(x)={}&(x-4)(x+4)^{15}(x-1)^8\\
 &\cdot(x^2+4x-4)^5(x^2+4x-2)^5\\
 &\cdot(x^4-88x^3-125x^2+1740x-778).
\end{aligned}
\]

The verifier reconstructs `H` by deleting `P_{0,0}` and `P_{0,2}`, checks
the five cell sizes and every quotient row sum, and recomputes this full
characteristic polynomial over the integers.

## 9. Literature status

The closest located papers concern distance spectra of the intact minimal
cages, adjacency spectra of vertex-deleted walk-regular graphs, graph
perturbations, and second-subconstituent modules. No direct source stating the
ordinary distance spectrum above was located. This is not a proof of novelty;
priority remains unresolved pending direct MathSciNet/zbMATH and
Terwilliger-literature checks.
